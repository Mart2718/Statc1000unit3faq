Distributions & Foundations

S13 · S14 · 5 questions

The answer

It depends on which parameter you are estimating — they address two different situations.

For means (t-interval / t-test): Use the n > 30 guideline. The CLT tells us the sampling distribution of x̄ is approximately normal when n is large enough, even if the population is skewed.

For proportions (z-interval / z-test): Use the np ≥ 10 and nq ≥ 10 condition. This checks whether you have enough successes and failures to justify a normal approximation.

🧠 Memory tip: Means → think "sample size" (n > 30). Proportions → think "successes and failures" (np ≥ 10 and nq ≥ 10).
⚠️ Common mistake: Applying the np ≥ 10 condition to a means problem, or using n > 30 as the normality check for a proportion. The two conditions are not interchangeable — match the condition to the parameter you're estimating.

The answer

The true population mean μ is a fixed number — it doesn't move around. It either IS or IS NOT in your specific interval. There is no "chance" involved once the interval is computed.

The 95% refers to the method, not the specific interval. If you repeated this process with many samples, about 95% of those intervals would capture the true mean.

✓ Correct interpretation: "We are 95% confident that the true mean is between [L, U]." The confidence is in the process, not a probability claim about this one interval.
⚠️ Common mistake: Writing "there is a 95% probability the mean is in this interval" on an exam. This will cost you points — the parameter is fixed, not random. The randomness comes from the sample, not the parameter.

The answer

Confidence Intervals: Used when you want to estimate a parameter. "What is a plausible range of values for the true mean?" The output is an interval.

Hypothesis Tests: Used when you want to test a specific claim. "Is there evidence the mean is greater than 70?" The output is a conclusion (reject or fail to reject).

Connection: A CI and a two-tailed hypothesis test are linked — if the hypothesized value falls outside the CI, you would reject H₀ at the corresponding significance level.
⚠️ Common mistake: Running a hypothesis test when the question asks you to "estimate" something, or building a CI when the question contains a specific claim to evaluate. Key words like "estimate the true proportion" → CI; "test the claim that" → hypothesis test.

The answer

The margin of error for a one-sample t-interval is: ME = t* × (s / √n)

Where t* is the critical value from the t-distribution (based on confidence level and df = n − 1), s is your sample standard deviation, and n is your sample size. The full interval is x̄ ± ME.

🧠 Larger sample → smaller ME. As n grows, the √n in the denominator gets larger, shrinking the margin of error — more data = more precision.
⚠️ Common mistake: Using z* (like 1.96) instead of t* for a means interval, or forgetting to look up t* with df = n − 1 (not df = n). Also: using the population standard deviation σ when only s is given.

Choosing the Right Test

S15 · S19 · 4 questions

📊 Quick Reference — Which Test Do I Use?

Scenario CluesUse This TestBecause…
Categorical data (Yes/No, Pass/Fail)Z-Test / Z-IntervalYou're dealing with a proportion p
Quantitative data (weight, pulse, price)T-Test / T-IntervalYou're dealing with a mean μ, σ unknown
Before/after same person; spouses; paired cities; twinsPaired T-TestDependent data — analyze the differences
Two separate, unrelated groupsTwo-Sample T or ZIndependent data — compare the two groups

The answer

In real-world statistics, you almost never know the population standard deviation σ. The practical rule:

Working with means? → Use the t-distribution. Always. (Unless the problem explicitly states σ is known — rare outside textbook examples.)

Working with proportions? → Use the z-distribution. The standard error formula uses p̂, which you have.

Simple rule: Proportions → Z. Means → T. The only exception is means with known σ, which almost never happens in practice.
⚠️ Common mistake: Seeing a large sample size (like n = 200) and switching to a Z-test for means because "the t-distribution approaches z at large n." That reasoning is technically true but practically irrelevant — always use the t-distribution for means unless σ is given.

The answer

The key question: Is each value in Group 1 directly linked to exactly one value in Group 2?

Hotel A's price on Expedia and Hotel A's price on Booking.com are both about Hotel A — they're a natural pair. A nicer hotel will be expensive on both platforms. By taking the difference within each pair, we remove that hotel-to-hotel variation and focus purely on the platform price difference.

🧠 Ask yourself: "Can I draw a line connecting each observation in Group 1 to exactly one observation in Group 2?" If yes → paired. If the groups are randomly assembled with no natural link → independent.
⚠️ Common mistake: Running a two-sample t-test on paired data. This ignores the natural connection between pairs and reduces the power of your test. When data is paired, running the wrong test can lead you to a completely different conclusion.

The answer

Proportion (Z): Asks about a fraction, percent, or rate. Look for: "what percent…", "what proportion…", "how many out of…", Yes/No responses, Pass/Fail counts.

Mean (T): Asks about an average of a measurement. Look for: "what is the average…", "estimate the mean…", measurements like weight, height, income, time, score, temperature.

⚠️ Common mistake: Getting fooled by a number that looks like a proportion but isn't — e.g., "the average score was 0.82 out of 1.0." That 0.82 is still a mean (of a score), not a proportion, so it needs a t-procedure. Ask: what did we measure on each person?

Hypothesis Testing & P-Values

S16 · S17 · 6 questions

The answer

Look at H₁ (the alternative hypothesis — what you're trying to show evidence for):

Two-tailed (H₁: μ ≠ value): Keywords: "different from," "has changed," "is not equal to."

One-tailed (H₁: μ > value or < value): Keywords: "greater than," "more than," "increased," "less than," "decreased," "improved."

🧠 Keyword check: "Different" = Two-tailed (≠). "Greater / Less / More / Fewer / Increased / Decreased" = One-tailed.
⚠️ Common mistake: Writing H₁ as a two-tailed test ("≠") when the research question clearly has a direction. A one-tailed test has more power when you have a directional prediction — using two-tailed when one-tailed is justified makes it harder to detect the effect.

The answer

If p-value < α → Reject H₀: "There IS sufficient evidence to support the claim that [H₁ in context]."

If p-value ≥ α → Fail to Reject H₀: "There is NOT sufficient evidence to support the claim that [H₁ in context]."

🎵 Memory rhyme: "If the P is low, the Null must go!" Small p-value = reject the null = sufficient evidence.
⚠️ Common mistake: Forgetting to write the conclusion in context. "Reject H₀" alone is never a complete conclusion — you must state what that decision means for the real-world claim. Always reference the variable, the population, and the direction of the finding.

The answer

The p-value is the probability of obtaining a test statistic as extreme as (or more extreme than) yours, assuming H₀ is true.

Think of it as: "If H₀ were true, how surprising is my data?" A small p-value = surprising = reason to doubt H₀. A large p-value = not surprising = no strong reason to reject H₀.

Important: The p-value is NOT the probability that H₀ is true, and NOT the probability you made an error. It is only the probability of observing your data (or more extreme) assuming H₀ is true.
⚠️ Common mistake: Saying "the p-value is 0.03, so there's a 3% chance the null hypothesis is true." This is a fundamental misinterpretation. The null is either true or false — it's not a random event with a probability.

The answer

Think of it like a court verdict. A jury that acquits someone says "Not Guilty" — not "Innocent." The difference matters:

"Not Guilty" = lack of evidence to convict (this is "Fail to Reject")

"Innocent" = proven they didn't do it (this would be "Accept H₀" — and we can never prove that)

🧠 Court analogy: "Fail to Reject H₀" = "Not Guilty." We lacked evidence, not proof of innocence.
⚠️ Common mistake: Writing "we accept H₀" or "we proved H₀ is true." This will lose you points — we can never prove a null hypothesis. We can only fail to find sufficient evidence against it.

The answer

Type I Error
False Positive
Rejecting H₀ when it's actually true. Probability = α (the significance level you set).
Type II Error
False Negative
Failing to reject H₀ when it's actually false. Probability = β. Power = 1 − β.

Medical example: H₀: Patient does NOT have the disease. Type I = telling a healthy patient they're sick (unnecessary treatment). Type II = telling a sick patient they're healthy (missing a real disease — often more dangerous).

⚠️ Common mistake: Swapping the two types. Remember: Type I error occurs when you incorrectly reject (reject a true null). Lower α → fewer Type I errors, but more Type II errors. There's always a trade-off.

The answer

Order affects the sign of your result but not your conclusion. Switching the order flips the sign of the test statistic and interval, but the p-value stays the same.

We don't use absolute values because direction matters. A difference of +12 means Group 1 is higher; −12 means Group 2 is higher. The sign tells you which group had the larger value — which is part of interpreting results in context.

🧠 Tip: Decide your order before running the test and write it into your hypotheses (e.g., H₁: μ₁ − μ₂ > 0). Stay consistent throughout.
⚠️ Common mistake: Switching the group order mid-problem — e.g., computing the test statistic with Group 1 first, then writing the confidence interval with Group 2 first. This creates a sign mismatch that contradicts your own hypotheses. Pick an order, commit to it everywhere.

The answer

One-sample t-test: df = n − 1

Paired t-test: df = n − 1, where n = number of pairs

Two-sample t-test: df is complex (Welch's formula). Let ArtOfStat handle this one.

Why subtract 1? You "lose" one degree of freedom because your sample data was used to estimate μ. That estimation uses up one piece of information.

🧠 Simple picture: 5 numbers that must average to 10 — once you know 4 of them, the 5th is forced. You only had 4 "free" choices. That's df = n − 1 = 4.
⚠️ Common mistake: Using df = n (forgetting the −1), or for paired tests, using the total number of observations rather than the number of pairs. For paired, n is the number of pairs — if you have 15 before-after measurements, df = 15 − 1 = 14, not 29.

The answer

Use z* when estimating a proportion (p), or the rare case where σ is known.

Use t* when estimating a mean and σ is unknown (almost always in practice).

The t* value is always slightly larger than z* at the same confidence level — it accounts for extra uncertainty from estimating σ with s. As df → ∞, t* → z*.

Easy rule: "Estimating a mean and σ not given → use t*. Estimating a proportion → use z*."
⚠️ Common mistake: Using z* = 1.96 for a t-interval just because it's a familiar number. The t* for 95% confidence with small df can be quite different — e.g., with df = 10, t* ≈ 2.228. Always look up t* with the correct degrees of freedom.

The answer

1. Rounding accumulation: When you round intermediate values (test statistic to 2 decimal places), that small error compounds when looking up the p-value. ArtOfStat stores 15+ decimal places internally.
